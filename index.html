How it's built and why it matters.

The Pitch

Acupressure wristbands for nausea have been sold in pharmacies for decades, and dedicated relief hardware runs $100 or more. The idea is simple: gentle, steady stimulation at the P6 point on your inner wrist. Your Apple Watch already sits exactly there.

Queasy turns that hardware into an app. Answer three quick questions, and it plays a calming vibration pattern on your wrist — for a bumpy flight, a car ride, a rough morning, or a wave of dizziness. One app covers what a shelf of single-purpose bands and remedies would.

What Mattered

Wrist-first, not phone-first. A nausea band lives on your wrist, so the watch is the primary interface. The iPhone is a fallback for people without a watch, using the same pattern engine.

Zero fuss when you feel awful. Nobody wants to read a manual mid-nausea. Three taps to a running pattern is the whole flow, on purpose.

Honest about what it is. Queasy is a comfort aid, not a medical device. The copy makes no treatment claims, and the app says so plainly.

Private by default. How you feel is personal. No accounts, no analytics, no network calls for your data — it stays on your devices.

Queasy is a drug-free comfort aid based on acupressure techniques whose clinical evidence is mixed. It does not diagnose, treat, or cure any condition, and results vary from person to person. If nausea is severe or persistent, comes with other symptoms, or you can't keep fluids down, please see a doctor.
